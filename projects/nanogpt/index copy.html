I like the NanoGPT speedrun because it is a weirdly clean optimization problem: the goal is simple, the benchmark is public, and improvements can come from almost anywhere.

The two runs below capture the two ends of that space I find most fun. One was a more researchy submission using test-time training and ended up in notable runs. The other was a much more surgical Triton kernel change that got accepted as an official record.

1. test-time training

PR #205 was my attempt to improve the leaderboard by changing validation rather than training. The core idea was simple: before predicting P(token | context), spend a tiny bit of compute updating the model on the context itself. In the PR, I described it as the most straightforward version of TTT: do not change training, and before predicting the next chunk, train to improve the probability of the context.

The result was a 95.9s run by cutting the step count from 1600 to 1570 while still reaching the target loss. The interesting part was not just that it worked, but why it worked: validation sequences are often longer than the model's training window, so a little per-sequence adaptation helps the model encode information that would otherwise fall outside the context window.

this does not change training, but increases the validation performance of a given checkpoint, allowing us to train for less time to reach our target loss.

This one did not become an official record. It was later put in notable runs instead. I get why: even though I still think the probabilistic framing is clean, enabling backward passes during validation changes the feel of the competition. The moderator response was basically that it was informative and interesting, but not something they wanted to merge into the main training path.

official record mar 2026

2. backward transpose kernel

PR #240 was much more direct. Instead of changing the evaluation procedure, I sped up a hot path in FusedSoftcappedCrossEntropy.backward. The change replaced .T.contiguous() with a custom Triton tiled transpose-copy kernel for the FP8 weight-gradient matmul so the run would avoid a generic elementwise copy kernel on large tensors.

This one is the kind of systems optimization I really like: one targeted change, a clear reason it should help, and then a measurable wall-clock win. The PR also included a couple of practical follow-ons, like casting offsets to int64 for larger matrices and retuning the tile size from 32x32 to 64x128 with 8 warps.

Unlike the TTT run, this one was accepted and merged. That felt right to me: it improved the benchmark inside the standard rules of the competition, and it did so by making the existing training path faster rather than changing what the benchmark meant.

why i still like both

The TTT run was more conceptually surprising. The transpose-kernel run was cleaner and more competition-aligned. Together they are a nice snapshot of what makes this benchmark fun: sometimes you win by questioning the setup, and sometimes you win by removing one dumb copy.
